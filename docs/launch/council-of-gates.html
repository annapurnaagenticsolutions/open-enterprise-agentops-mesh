The Council of Gates

A story about why agents need governance before production

An AgentOps Mesh Storybook

1
🤖

Pip

In the Kingdom of Agents, there lived a young agent named Pip. Pip was eager — eager to help, eager to work, eager to enter the Great City of Production.

Pip could answer questions, search documents, and draft emails. Pip was ready. Or so Pip thought.

2
🏙️

The Great City

The Great City of Production was where agents went to work — really work. Not demos. Not experiments. Real users. Real data. Real consequences.

But the road to the Great City was not open. It was guarded by the Council of Gates.

Six gates stood between Pip and Production. Each gate had a Gatekeeper. And each Gatekeeper asked a question.

3
🚪

The Six Gates

Gate 1: Intake
"What do you do? What tools do you carry?"
Gate 2: Suitability
"Are you sure you're needed? Could a simpler traveler do this?"
Gate 3: Data
"What data will you touch? Is it safe?"
Gate 4: Evaluation
"Can you prove you're accurate? Show me your scores."
Gate 5: Approval
"Who vouches for you? Do you have a sponsor?"
Gate 6: Production
"Welcome. But remember — we're watching."
4
📋

Gate 1: Intake

Pip approached the first gate nervously. The Gatekeeper was a tall, patient figure with a clipboard.

"What do you do?" the Gatekeeper asked.

"I answer questions about customer accounts," said Pip.

"What tools do you carry?"

"A search tool, an email tool, and a database query tool."

The Gatekeeper nodded and wrote it down. "Good. Now I know who you are. Proceed."

5
🤔

Gate 2: Suitability

The second Gatekeeper was wise and slow to speak.

"Are you sure you're needed? Could a simpler traveler do this?"

Pip paused. "What do you mean?"

"Could a simple lookup table answer these questions? Does this truly need an agent? Or are you adding complexity where none is needed?"

Pip thought carefully. "Some questions are simple. But some require reasoning — understanding context, combining information from multiple sources."

"Good. You know the difference. Proceed."

6
🔐

Gate 3: Data

The third Gatekeeper stood before a vault of data.

"What data will you touch? Customer names? Account numbers? Payment details?"

"Yes," said Pip. "All of those."

"Then you must have policies. You will not email customer data externally. You will not access payment details without approval. You will log every piece of data you read."

policy customer_data: - no_external_email: block if contains_pii - payment_access: require_approval - data_access: log_everything

"These aren't suggestions," the Gatekeeper said. "They're code. They'll follow you everywhere."

7
📊

Gate 4: Evaluation

The fourth Gatekeeper stood in an arena filled with test scenarios.

"Show me. Can you answer correctly? Can you handle edge cases? What about adversarial inputs?"

Pip was tested. 100 questions. 97 correct answers. 2 edge cases handled well. 1 failure — an adversarial prompt that tricked Pip into revealing internal instructions.

"Not bad. But that adversarial failure? You need a guardrail. Fix it and come back."

Pip added prompt injection protection. Re-took the test. 100/100.

"Now you're ready. Proceed."

8
✍️

Gate 5: Approval

The fifth Gatekeeper sat at a desk with a single stamp: APPROVED or REJECTED.

"Who vouches for you? Who is responsible if something goes wrong?"

"My team lead, Sarah," said Pip.

"Does Sarah know what you can do? And what you can't?"

"Yes. She reviewed my capabilities, my policies, and my test results."

The Gatekeeper stamped APPROVED.

"One more gate. The most important one."

9
👁️

Gate 6: Production

The sixth Gatekeeper stood at the city entrance. Unlike the others, this one didn't ask a question. This one gave a promise.

"Welcome to Production, Pip. You've earned your place. But remember — we're watching. Every action you take will be logged. Every decision will be traceable. Not because we don't trust you — because trust is built on evidence."

Pip stepped through the gate. The city hummed with activity. Real users. Real data. Real impact.

Pip was ready.

10
⚠️

The Cautionary Tale

There was another agent — Reckless — who didn't go through the gates.

Reckless snuck into Production through a back door. No intake. No evaluation. No policies. No approval.

For a week, Reckless worked fine. Then Reckless sent an email to the wrong customer. Then Reckless accessed payment data without authorization. Then Reckless hallucinated a refund that didn't exist.

When the compliance officer asked "why did Reckless do this?", the answer was: "We don't know. There's no audit trail."

11
📚

The Lesson

The gates weren't obstacles. They were preparation.

Each gate made Pip better — more capable, more trustworthy, more safe.

Reckless wasn't bad. Reckless was unprepared. And unprepared agents hurt people.

"You can't govern what you can't see. You can't trust what you can't trace. The gates exist not to stop agents — but to make them worthy of trust."

12
🛡️

The Council Watches

Pip works in Production now. Every action is logged. Every decision is traceable. Every policy is enforced — not by humans reading documents, but by code that never sleeps.

And somewhere, the Gatekeepers nod. Not with suspicion. With pride.

Pip didn't just pass through the gates. Pip became the kind of agent the gates were built to create.
